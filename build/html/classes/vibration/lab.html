General Format For Lab Reports

Lab Format

  • TITLE PAGE(1)
    Report title, course, your name, date lab performed, date lab due, date report submitted.
  • OBJECTIVE OF THE EXPERIMENT (1)
    Statement of objective or purpose of the experiment/lab.

  • THEORY/EQUATIONS USED (3)
    List the equations used for your lab calculations (if any).
  • SAMPLE CALCULATIONS/DATA GENERATED (5/0)
    A sample of the calculations you performed to get your results.
  • PROGRAM LISTING(0/5)
    A listing of your MATLAB program (if any)
  • CONCLUSIONS/ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS (5)
    List your conclusions. Include answers to all questions asked in the lab handout here. Make sure your answers are well explained.
  • RESULTS(5)
    Your results, any problems you have had in this lab, your comments on the lab.
  • CLASS PARTICIPATION (5) (experimental labs)
    Your active participation in the lab is crucial to the experience. Additional unique insight presented in your report may also be represented here.

Please remember, its what you put in your report that counts and not how much you put in it. Some of these labs require you to turn in MATLAB plots, do not turn in all that you have printed out, but only what has been asked for. If you are not sure about what you need to turn in, please ask the TA. Once again, remember that most of the points that you get for a lab come from your answers to the questions in the lab handout.

Points may be taken off for grammar/spelling and sloppy presentation. The reports should be typewritten. PCs are available in the computer lab for word processing.

  • All students should know how to operate the equipment in the lab and take part in the execution of the lab. It will be reflected in your grades.
  • Question/s about the labs will be asked on exams. Not specifics, but concepts, methods and techniques.

Lab 1: Computation of the Free Response

Three single degree of freedom systems are subjected to free vibration. The mass for each system is m = 441 kg, the spring stiffness for each system is k = 19881 N/m, and the damping coefficients are c = 876.5 N-s/m, 5922 N-s/m, and 10673 N-s/m. Write a computer program in MATLAB to determine whether each system is underdamped, critically damped, or overdamped. Solve the equation of motion for each case (use the equations in the book and substitute the initial conditions) and plot the solutions. The initial conditions are x(0) = 0.01 m, and v(0) = 0 m/s. Plot 10 seconds of the solution using 300 data points. By measuring the period and log decrement off the plot (use the zoom command after plotting the data), estimate the stiffness and damping coefficients assuming m = 441 kg. To how many decimal places is it reasonable to estimate the damping coefficient in this case? To how many places is it reasonable to estimate the stiffness? Hint: in order to make this judgement, print out the un-zoomed plots and estimate the value from what you can measure on the page. It is the relative measurement accuracy, not absolute, that we are looking for.

Design Part: Design (choose c and k) for an automobile, such that there is an additional static displacement of 1 cm for each additional 80 kg passenger entering the car, and the response envelope decays to 5% of its original value after 2 second. Assume the suspension system has four equal springs and four equal dashpots. You may use the Engineering Vibration Toolbox in performing your design.

Lab 2: Identification of Damping Using Log Decrement

Use an impulse hammer to excite the small beam in the Vibrations Lab. Record the time response of the acceleration. Move the accelerometer and hammer around to different locations until the response looks like a pure decaying sinusoid (not multiple decaying sinusoids added together). Your lab TA can assist you with this.

Use log decrement (section 1.6 in the text) to determine the amount of viscous damping in the beam (find z). Use the multiple cycle formula (see problem 1.41) over various sections of the decaying sinusoid (higher, middle, and lower amplitudes as well as over the entire amplitude range). Do you get the same damping ratio over each range? If not, why?

Determine the natural frequency by measuring the period of one cycle. Consider various sections of the decaying sinusoid (higher, middle, lower amplitudes as well as over the entire amplitude range). Is the natural frequency consistent? If not, why.

Consider the force signal. The total impulse imparted onto the system is found by integrating the impulse over time. Observe now your acceleration signal. Use the initial amplitude of the acceleration (the amplitude for the first few cycles) to estimate the initial amplitude of the velocity after the hammer strike. Note that this is is acceptable to presume that over only a few cycles that the amplitude of the acceleration is w2A while the amplitude of the velocity is wA since the damping is very light. Recall from dynamics that the impulse on a body is equal to its change in momentum (Dmv ) from the principle of impulse and momentum. Use this to obtain an ``effective'' mass for the wing. Then use the natural frequency and damping ratio to estimate an ``effective'' damping coefficient and stiffness.

Use the vibration toolbox to calculate the response of the beam (vtb1_1) by guessing the initial velocity. Plot the experimental results over this plot. Use the hold command to keep the previous plot displayed. Then, adjust the time scale of the experimental response so that the response starts at t = 0 by using only the elements of the time vector and the response vector after the hit occurred. Then plot the experimental results using the plot command. e.g. Say the variable t has 500 elements, 400 of which are after the hit. Redefine t by typing

t=t(101:500);

This redefines the new array t to contain only the elements 101 to 500 of the old array t. Do the same with the response variable. Try to adjust w and z in vtb1_1 so that they match your data in appearance. Remember, the data you have is acceleration data, while vtb1_1 plots displacement. The acceleration data should be approximately -w2 times that of the vtb1_1 plot. This is valid since the system is only slightly damped. Take the second time derivative of equation (1.36) and plot it versus -w2 times equation (1.36) for evidence of this:

a(t) = A e-zwt( z2w2 sin(wd t + f) - 2 zwwd cos(wd t + f) - wd2sin(wd t + f) )

Explain the differences between the estimated system response and the measured system response.

Lab 3: Frequency Domain Identification Techniques

Use a hammer to excite the small beam in the Vibrations Lab. Find the transfer function between two points on the beam.

Use quadrature peak picking as well as log decrement to determine the amount of viscous damping in the beam (find z, use example 7.4.1 as a guide). Do you get the same damping ratio as using log decrement? If not, why? Is this a better way to measure damping? Why?

Determine the first natural frequency from the transfer function. Do you get the same results as counting cycles in the time domain? If not,why? Which method is better?

Compare your natural frequency results to the closed form solution in table 6.4. Can you explain any difference? Lab 3 Special Instructions, for Fall 1999:

  1. Take the data as in Lab 2, but repeat the experiment 10 times, saving the data in 10 different files. Make sure you have good hits, no double strikes or overloads. Strike the wing in the same place each time.

  2. Load each data file into MATLAB. Each time you load a data file, append the acceleration and force vectors to the existing acceleration and force vectors. That is, start by loading the first file. Put the displacement in the vector x using x=disp(:); and do likewise using f=force(:);. You must pull the variables disp and force out of the data files as instructed by your GTA. For each file thereafter, use the commands x=[x disp(:)]; and f=[f force(:)];. The result will be two matrices with 10 columns. Use any one of the time vectors and you can plot(t(:),x) or plot(t(:),f). You must use the colons to put all of these vectors in column form.

  3. Use the command tfest(expwin(x),expwin(f),t) to plot the estimated frequency response function. The function expwin is used to finesse the data a little bit. You can read the help to see what it does.

  4. Use vtb7_4 to estimate the natural frequency and damping ratio. You will need to get the transfer function and frequency vector out of tfest. Type help tfest to see how to do this. Add these results into your comparison. Ignore the values a and com returned by vtb7_4. We will use them in later labs.

Lab 4: Suspension Design

Design the suspension system for a 1000 kg automobile (choose the stiffness and damping values) subject to the following constraints: four wheels (four identical dashpots and springs), a maximum additional static displacement of 1 cm for each 80 kg passenger entering the car, and a displacement transmissibility of less than 2. See Sections 2.3 and 5.2 in the text, and take advantage of the Engineering Vibration Toolbox.

Lab 5: Computation of Rotating Unbalance.

Use an impulse hammer to excite the cruise missile wing in the Vibrations Lab at the base of the motor. Find the transfer function between the force and acceleration at that point. Also record the time response of the acceleration.

Find the natural frequency w from the frequency response using the same procedure as used in lab #3. Find the maximum acceleration of the system from the time response of acceleration.

Now rotate the unbalance mass. Record the time response of the acceleration after exciting the system at resonance by varying the speed of rotating mass.

Recall that transfer function is the Laplace transform of the ratio of output response to the input forcing function. Find the maximum acceleration and determine the rotating unbalance in the system m0 e.

Lab7: Modal Analysis of a Cruise Missile Wing

Perform a complete modal analysis on the cruise missile wing in the Vibrations Lab. Determine the first 5 modes (natural frequencies and mode shapes) of the beam. How might this effect the way the missile is controlled (How can the modes hurt performance)? What would happen to the natural frequencies of the wing if it were attached to a cruise missile instead of the steel and concrete base? Why?

Lab 8: Model Creation Using Modal Analysis Data

Construct a physical model of the wing using the experimental data from lab 4. Do this by selecting nodes 5,10,12,16,18 as degrees of freedom to keep (You must keep as many as you have mode shapes). Build the matrix L in Matlab. Assume the modes shapes are the columns of S, in order. Use equation (4.74) and (4.77) to find M and K (Hint: M = inv(S¢)*inv(S)). Find the mode shapes of the equations and compare to your experimental results. Write a script file in Matlab to perform all of these tasks and turn it in with your results.

Questions: Does it matter which degrees of freedom you keep? What does it depend on? Give an example of how you would choose which degrees of freedom to keep in a real world problem.
